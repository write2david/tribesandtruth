I’ve been reflecting on a simple truth lately: **The hardest part of almost any task is just getting started.** Once you establish momentum, it’s much easier to keep going.


But why do we hesitate at the starting line? **Because we love what is comfortable.** It is comfortable to skip the pushups, stay out of the cold plunge, sleep in, or avoid studying.

The key isn't pretending the friction isn't there. **The key is to acknowledge our love for comfort, push back against it, and proactively plan how to overcome its influence.** Growth requires us to intentionally choose discomfort over convenience. Ultimately, this is the ultimate test of maturity: **not letting our love of comfort dictate our decisions, because if we let it, it absolutely will.**

There is an old saying that fits perfectly here: *Discomfort is inevitable, but suffering is optional.* The cold water or the hard work is going to feel uncomfortable no matter what—but the mental agonizing, procrastinating, and dreading? That's the optional suffering we inflict on ourselves.

Think about standing on the edge of a cold pool. The anticipation is always worse than the reality. I recently saw a hilarious mat for a cold plunge that perfectly nails this feeling. It reads:

> *"Thinking about it longer won't make the water warmer."*


The decision lives in the space between a stimulus and our response.

Stimulus = phone notification reminding me to do 15 pushups.


ABILITY

Principle:  you can choose to do hard things.

Examples:  I've cold plunged dozens of time.  I've done over 10,000 in the last 9 months.  I've had my finger pricked over 100 times.  I've run two ultramarathons and two "century" (100+ mile) bike rides.


POWER OF COMFORT

Principle:  you skip discomfort because of your love for maintaining comfort.

Examples:  Not doing pushups when my phone notification prompts me.  Skipping the cold plunge.  Snoozing my early morning alarm.



SUFFERING

Principle:  "Discomfort is inevitable, but suffering is optional."  If you are okay with the discomfort, then it's not suffering.

Examples:  After your neighbor is in a devastating car accident, mowing their lawn is hardly a sacrifice.  After 100 finger-pricks, you know the pain is over quickly and it's not worth dreading.  


CHOOSING RIGHT = MATURITY

Principle:  "Agonizing about the water temperature doesn't make the cold plunge any warmer." The desire for personal comfort will create immature decisions unless we learn to ignore or overcome it.


GOOD START = GOOD ENDINGS

Principle:  The primary friction happens at the starting line. The hard part is getting started.  

Examples:  If you get in the car to go to the gym, finishing the workout will happen naturally.  If you can jump into the pool, then your body will get used to the water temperature after a few minutes. 




But here’s the trick: Once I actually start, I never quit early.

It’s the same story with going to the gym, driving hours to a conference, or pushing through a 100-mile bike ride or ultra-marathon. The friction is entirely at the starting line. We often let the "urgent" crowd out the "important," but the truly important things in life require long-term, intentional decisions.

### The Power in the Pause

In the book *The Five Types of Wealth*, there’s a powerful discussion about human maturity: **Our true power lies in the tiny space between a stimulus and our response.** That pause is where your decision lives, and it's exactly where you have to execute your plan to fight comfort.

* **With Money:** Receiving cash (stimulus) vs. pausing to save it rather than immediately spending it on short-term comfort (response).
* **With Relationships:** Someone pushing your buttons (stimulus) vs. pausing to intentionally de-escalate rather than snapping back in the heat of the moment (response).

### Overcoming the Friction

I see this in my own life daily. I’ve done about 10,000 pushups this year, but when my phone app pings me to do a set of 15, I often *really* don't feel like it.

But here’s the trick: **Once I actually start, I never quit early.**

It’s the same story with going to the gym, driving hours to a conference, or pushing through a 100-mile bike ride or ultra-marathon. The friction is entirely at the starting line because your brain is trying to protect its comfort zone. We often let the "urgent" crowd out the "important," but the truly important things in life require us to proactively plan for that friction and choose to step into the cold.

The next time you get a notification to work out or find yourself hesitating on a goal, don't swipe it away and don't overthink it. Bridge that gap between the stimulus and the response. Recognize the urge to stay comfortable, execute your plan to push past it, and take the first step—once you’re in motion, you’ll be completely fine.
